Tribute to Dr. Norman Borlaug

Dr. Norman Borlaug, third from the left, trains biologists in Mexico on how to increase wheat yields - part of his lifelong war on hunger.

Dr. Norman Ernest Borlaug (1914 - 2009) was an American agronomist and humanitarian who led initiatives worldwide that contributed significantly to increased agricultural production.

Here are some key milestones in his remarkable life:

  • 1914: Born in Cresco, Iowa
  • 1933: Leaves his family's farm to attend the University of Minnesota
  • 1935: Works in the Civilian Conservation Corps during the Great Depression
  • 1937: Graduates from the University of Minnesota and joins the US Forestry Service
  • 1938: Marries Margaret Gibson and returns to school to study under Elvin Charles Stakman
  • 1942: Receives a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plant Pathology
  • 1944: Heads a plant pathology program in Mexico, breeding disease-resistant wheat
  • 1953: Develops a high-yielding strain of wheat that transforms agriculture in Mexico
  • 1962: Introduces high-yielding wheat strains to India, averting famine
  • 1970: Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his contributions to world peace through agriculture
  • 1983: Helps increase maize and sorghum yields in seven African countries
  • 1984: Joins Texas A&M University as a distinguished professor
  • 2005: Advocates for genetically modified crops to meet growing food demands
  • 2009: Passes away at the age of 95, leaving behind a legacy of agricultural innovation and humanitarianism
